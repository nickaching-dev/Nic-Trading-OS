# Lesson 5: Accounts, Brokerages, and a Simple First Plan

## Goal

Understand where investing happens and how a beginner can create a basic plan before buying anything.

## Brokerage account

A brokerage account is the account that lets you buy and hold investments like stocks, ETFs, and mutual funds.

## Tax-advantaged accounts

If you are in the U.S., common account types include:

- 401(k) or similar employer retirement plan
- IRA
- Roth IRA
- HSA, if eligible
- taxable brokerage account

The account is the wrapper. The investments inside the account are the actual assets you own.

## A simple U.S. beginner funding order

This is a common educational framework, not a universal rule:

1. Build emergency reserves
2. Capture employer match if available
3. Pay off high-interest debt
4. Use tax-advantaged accounts that fit your situation
5. Use a taxable brokerage account for additional long-term investing

## Before your first investment

Be able to answer:

- What is this money for?
- When will I need it?
- How much volatility can I handle?
- Why am I choosing this investment?
- What does it cost?

## A simple first-plan template

- Goal: retirement, home down payment, financial independence, or another long-term goal
- Time horizon: short, medium, or long
- Account type: taxable or tax-advantaged
- Strategy: broad diversified funds or another clearly understood approach
- Contribution plan: one-time, monthly, or every paycheck
- Behavior rule: what you will do during market drops

## Automation

Automation is one of the most beginner-friendly investing tools.

Examples:

- automatic contributions from paycheck
- scheduled transfers into an investment account
- recurring purchases into chosen funds

## Beginner mistakes to avoid

- opening an account before choosing a strategy
- using a long-term stock portfolio for short-term goals
- ignoring taxes and account type
- buying something because it is trending online

## Quick check

1. What is the difference between an account and an investment?
2. Why is an employer match important if available?
3. What should you know before buying your first fund or stock?
