# Lesson 2: Stocks, Bonds, Funds, ETFs, and Index Funds

## Goal

Understand the main investing building blocks and why funds are often easier for beginners than individual stocks.

## Stocks

A stock is a share of ownership in a company.

Pros:

- high long-term growth potential
- easy to buy and sell in public markets

Cons:

- prices can fall a lot
- picking a few individual stocks adds concentration risk

## Bonds

A bond is a loan from investors to a government or company.

Pros:

- usually lower volatility than stocks
- can provide income and stability

Cons:

- lower expected return than stocks over long periods
- still carries risk, including interest-rate and credit risk

## Mutual funds

A mutual fund pools money from many investors and buys a collection of investments.

Key beginner fact:

- mutual funds usually trade once per day after the market closes

## ETFs

An ETF, or exchange-traded fund, is also a basket of investments, but it trades on an exchange during the day like a stock.

Key beginner fact:

- ETFs are often low-cost and easy to use for broad diversification

## Index funds

An index fund is a mutual fund or ETF that tries to track a market index instead of beat it through active stock picking.

Example idea:

- a broad U.S. stock index fund tries to own a wide slice of the U.S. stock market

## Why funds help beginners

A single broad fund can give you exposure to many companies at once. That reduces the damage from any one company doing badly.

That does not remove market risk, but it helps remove a lot of unnecessary single-stock risk.

## Simple mental model

- stock = one company
- bond = one loan
- fund = basket
- ETF = basket traded like a stock
- index fund = basket following rules instead of trying to outsmart the market

## Beginner mistakes to avoid

- thinking ETF automatically means safe
- thinking more holdings means zero risk
- buying many funds that all hold the same things
- buying individual stocks before understanding diversification
