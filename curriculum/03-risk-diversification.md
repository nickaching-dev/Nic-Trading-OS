# Lesson 3: Risk, Diversification, and Asset Allocation

## Goal

Learn how investors manage risk without pretending risk can be eliminated.

## Risk tolerance vs. risk capacity

Risk tolerance is how much market volatility you can emotionally handle.

Risk capacity is how much risk your financial situation can handle.

These are not always the same.

Example:

- You may feel brave enough for a 100% stock portfolio.
- But if you need the money in 3 years, your risk capacity may be low.

## Volatility

Volatility means prices move around, sometimes sharply.

Important beginner point:

- volatility is normal in stock investing
- volatility is uncomfortable, but discomfort is not the same as a broken strategy

## Diversification

Diversification means spreading your money across many investments so that one failure does not ruin your plan.

You can diversify:

- across companies
- across sectors
- across countries
- across asset types such as stocks and bonds

## Asset allocation

Asset allocation is how you split your portfolio among major asset types, usually stocks, bonds, and cash.

This is one of the biggest drivers of your investing experience.

In general:

- more stocks = more long-term growth potential and bigger ups and downs
- more bonds or cash = more stability and lower expected return

## Rebalancing

Rebalancing means bringing your portfolio back toward its target mix when markets move it away.

Example:

- if stocks rise a lot, they may become a larger share of your portfolio than intended
- rebalancing trims that drift

## The key beginner truth

The best portfolio is not the most aggressive one. It is the one you can stick with during scary markets.

## Beginner mistakes to avoid

- confusing bravery with suitability
- assuming a diversified portfolio never loses money
- choosing an allocation based only on recent market performance
- building a portfolio you will panic-sell in a downturn

## Quick check

2. What problem does diversification solve well?
3. Why might a 100% stock portfolio be wrong for some people even if stocks have higher expected returns?
